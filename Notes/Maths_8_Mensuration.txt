# COMPLETE MENSURATION GUIDE FOR MHT CET MCA ENTRANCE EXAM

## Exam Overview
- **Weightage**: 4-6 questions (8-12 marks)
- **Difficulty Level**: Easy to Moderate
- **Focus Areas**: Area of 2D shapes, Volume and Surface Area of 3D solids
- **Key Skill**: Formula memorization + Quick calculations
- **Time per question**: Ideally 45-60 seconds

# SECTION 1: FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS

## 1.1 Basic Definitions

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Perimeter** | Total length of the boundary of a closed figure | Fencing around a garden |
| **Area** | Measure of the surface enclosed by a closed figure | Carpet required for a room |
| **Volume** | Space occupied by a solid object | Water in a tank |
| **Surface Area** | Total area of all surfaces of a solid | Paint required for a box |
| **Lateral/Curved Surface Area** | Area of lateral/side surfaces (excluding top and bottom) | Curved part of a cylinder |
| **Cross-section** | Shape obtained by cutting perpendicular to length | Circular cross-section of cylinder |

## 1.2 Units of Measurement

| Quantity | Common Units | Conversion Facts |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Length | mm, cm, m, km | 1 m = 100 cm, 1 km = 1000 m |
| Area | mm², cm², m², km², hectare | 1 m² = 10,000 cm², 1 hectare = 10,000 m² |
| Volume | mm³, cm³, m³, litre | 1 litre = 1000 cm³ = 0.001 m³ |

**Important Conversions to Memorize:**
- 1 m = 100 cm
- 1 m² = 10,000 cm²
- 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³
- 1 litre = 1000 cm³
- 1 hectare = 10,000 m²
- 1 acre = 4046.86 m² (rarely asked, but good to know)

## 1.3 Important Mathematical Constants

| Constant | Value | When to Use |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| π (pi) | 22/7 or 3.14 | Circle, cylinder, cone, sphere problems |
| √2 | 1.414 | Square diagonal, cube face diagonal |
| √3 | 1.732 | Equilateral triangle height, cube diagonal |
| √5 | 2.236 | Occurs in some geometry problems |

**MHT CET Tip:** Usually π = 22/7 is used for easier calculations. If options are in decimals, use 3.14.

# SECTION 2: TRIANGLES - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 2.1 General Triangle (Scalene)

### Formulas:
- **Area** = ½ × base × height
- **Perimeter** = a + b + c (sum of all sides)
- **Semi-perimeter (s)** = (a + b + c)/2

### Heron's Formula (When height is not given)
**Area** = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

### Important Properties:
- Sum of angles = 180°
- Exterior angle = Sum of interior opposite angles
- Triangle inequality: Sum of any two sides > Third side

### Solved Examples:

**Example 1**: Find area of triangle with sides 13 cm, 14 cm, 15 cm.
```
s = (13+14+15)/2 = 42/2 = 21 cm
Area = √[21(21-13)(21-14)(21-15)]
     = √[21 × 8 × 7 × 6]
     = √[21 × 336]
     = √7056 = 84 cm²

Shortcut: 13-14-15 is a known triplet → area = 84 cm²
```

**Example 2**: Find area of triangle with base 12 cm and height 8 cm.
```
Area = ½ × 12 × 8 = 48 cm²
```

## 2.2 Right-Angled Triangle

### Formulas:
- **Area** = ½ × (product of legs containing right angle)
- **Hypotenuse** = √(base² + height²) (Pythagoras theorem)
- **Perimeter** = base + height + hypotenuse

### Pythagorean Triplets (Memorize for speed):
| Triplet | Check |
|---------|-------|
| 3-4-5 | 3²+4² = 9+16=25=5² |
| 5-12-13 | 5²+12² = 25+144=169=13² |
| 7-24-25 | 7²+24² = 49+576=625=25² |
| 8-15-17 | 8²+15² = 64+225=289=17² |
| 9-40-41 | 9²+40² = 81+1600=1681=41² |
| 12-35-37 | 12²+35² = 144+1225=1369=37² |

### Solved Examples:

**Example 3**: In a right triangle, base = 6 cm, height = 8 cm. Find area and hypotenuse.
```
Area = ½ × 6 × 8 = 24 cm²
Hypotenuse = √(6² + 8²) = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10 cm
```

**Example 4**: Hypotenuse of right triangle is 13 cm, one side is 5 cm. Find area.
```
Using Pythagoras: 5² + b² = 13²
25 + b² = 169
b² = 144 → b = 12 cm
Area = ½ × 5 × 12 = 30 cm²
```

## 2.3 Equilateral Triangle

### Formulas:
| Measure | Formula | Derivation/Explanation |
|---------|---------|----------------------|
| Area | (√3/4) a² | From ½ × base × height |
| Perimeter | 3a | Sum of all sides |
| Height | (√3/2) a | Perpendicular from vertex to base |
| Inradius | a/(2√3) | Radius of inscribed circle |
| Circumradius | a/√3 | Radius of circumscribed circle |

### Important Points:
- All angles = 60°
- All sides equal
- Altitude, median, angle bisector are same line

### Solved Examples:

**Example 5**: Find area and height of equilateral triangle with side 12 cm.
```
Area = (√3/4) × 12² = (√3/4) × 144 = 36√3 cm²
Height = (√3/2) × 12 = 6√3 cm

Numerical value: 36 × 1.732 = 62.35 cm²
```

**Example 6**: If area of equilateral triangle is 16√3 cm², find its side.
```
(√3/4)a² = 16√3
a²/4 = 16
a² = 64 → a = 8 cm
```

## 2.4 Isosceles Triangle

### Formulas:
- **Area** = ½ × base × height
- **Perimeter** = 2a + b (where a = equal sides, b = base)
- **Height** = √(a² - (b/2)²)

### Solved Example:

**Example 7**: Isosceles triangle has equal sides 10 cm, base 12 cm. Find area.
```
Height = √(10² - 6²) = √(100 - 36) = √64 = 8 cm
Area = ½ × 12 × 8 = 48 cm²
```

## 2.5 Important Triangle Properties for MHT CET

### 1. Median of Triangle
- Divides triangle into two equal areas
- Length of median = ½√(2a² + 2b² - c²)

### 2. Angle Bisector Theorem
- Divides opposite side in ratio of adjacent sides

### 3. Area by Coordinate Geometry
**Area** = ½|x₁(y₂ - y₃) + x₂(y₃ - y₁) + x₃(y₁ - y₂)|

**Example 8**: Find area of triangle with vertices (0,0), (4,0), (0,3).
```
Area = ½|0(0-3) + 4(3-0) + 0(0-0)|
     = ½|0 + 12 + 0| = 6 sq units
```

### 4. MHT CET Shortcut - Triangle Area Ratios
If a line parallel to base divides triangle, areas are proportional to squares of corresponding sides.

# SECTION 3: QUADRILATERALS - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 3.1 Square

### Complete Formula Sheet:

| Measure | Formula | Alternative Form |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| Area | a² | (diagonal)²/2 |
| Perimeter | 4a | - |
| Diagonal | a√2 | √(2 × Area) |
| Side | a | √Area |
| Inradius | a/2 | - |
| Circumradius | a/√2 | - |

### Important Relations:
- Diagonal = side × √2
- Area = (diagonal)²/2
- Side = diagonal/√2

### Solved Examples:

**Example 9**: Find area and diagonal of square with side 10 cm.
```
Area = 10² = 100 cm²
Diagonal = 10√2 cm = 10 × 1.414 = 14.14 cm
```

**Example 10**: Diagonal of square is 12√2 cm. Find its area.
```
Side = diagonal/√2 = 12√2/√2 = 12 cm
Area = 12² = 144 cm²
Shortcut: Area = (diagonal)²/2 = (12√2)²/2 = (144×2)/2 = 144 cm²
```

## 3.2 Rectangle

### Complete Formula Sheet:

| Measure | Formula | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Area | l × b | - |
| Perimeter | 2(l + b) | - |
| Diagonal | √(l² + b²) | - |
| Length | Area/b | When breadth known |
| Breadth | Area/l | When length known |

### Important Points:
- Opposite sides equal and parallel
- All angles 90°
- Diagonals are equal and bisect each other

### Solved Examples:

**Example 11**: Find area and diagonal of rectangle with length 8 cm, breadth 6 cm.
```
Area = 8 × 6 = 48 cm²
Diagonal = √(8² + 6²) = √(64 + 36) = √100 = 10 cm
```

**Example 12**: Perimeter of rectangle is 40 cm, length is 12 cm. Find area.
```
2(l + b) = 40
2(12 + b) = 40
12 + b = 20 → b = 8 cm
Area = 12 × 8 = 96 cm²
```

**Example 13**: Diagonal of rectangle is 17 cm, length is 15 cm. Find area.
```
15² + b² = 17²
225 + b² = 289
b² = 64 → b = 8 cm
Area = 15 × 8 = 120 cm²
```

## 3.3 Parallelogram

### Formulas:
- **Area** = base × height
- **Perimeter** = 2(a + b) where a, b are adjacent sides
- **Height** = Area/base
- **Diagonals**: d₁² + d₂² = 2(a² + b²)

### Important Points:
- Opposite sides parallel and equal
- Opposite angles equal
- Adjacent angles supplementary (sum = 180°)
- Diagonals bisect each other

### Solved Examples:

**Example 14**: Find area of parallelogram with base 12 cm and height 8 cm.
```
Area = 12 × 8 = 96 cm²
```

**Example 15**: Adjacent sides of parallelogram are 10 cm and 12 cm, area is 60√3 cm². Find angle between sides.
```
Area = a × b × sin θ
60√3 = 10 × 12 × sin θ
60√3 = 120 sin θ
sin θ = √3/2 → θ = 60° or 120°
```

## 3.4 Rhombus

### Complete Formula Sheet:

| Measure | Formula | Alternative |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Area | ½ × d₁ × d₂ | a² sin θ |
| Perimeter | 4a | - |
| Side | a = ½√(d₁² + d₂²) | - |
| Height | Area/a | - |

### Important Properties:
- All sides equal
- Diagonals bisect at 90°
- Diagonals bisect angles
- Area = product of diagonals/2

### Solved Examples:

**Example 16**: Find area of rhombus with diagonals 10 cm and 12 cm.
```
Area = ½ × 10 × 12 = 60 cm²
Side = ½√(10² + 12²) = ½√(100 + 144) = ½√244 = ½ × 15.62 = 7.81 cm
```

**Example 17**: Side of rhombus is 10 cm, one diagonal is 12 cm. Find area.
```
Let d₁ = 12 cm
d₁/2 = 6 cm
From right triangle: (d₁/2)² + (d₂/2)² = a²
6² + (d₂/2)² = 10²
36 + (d₂/2)² = 100
(d₂/2)² = 64
d₂/2 = 8 → d₂ = 16 cm
Area = ½ × 12 × 16 = 96 cm²
```

## 3.5 Trapezium (Trapezoid)

### Formulas:
- **Area** = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height = ½ × (a + b) × h
- **Perimeter** = sum of all sides
- **Median** = ½(a + b) (line joining midpoints of non-parallel sides)

### Types of Trapezium:
1. **Isosceles Trapezium**: Non-parallel sides equal
2. **Right Trapezium**: One side perpendicular to parallel sides

### Solved Examples:

**Example 18**: Find area of trapezium with parallel sides 10 cm and 14 cm, height 8 cm.
```
Area = ½ × (10 + 14) × 8 = ½ × 24 × 8 = 96 cm²
```

**Example 19**: Area of trapezium is 120 cm², parallel sides are 8 cm and 12 cm. Find height.
```
½ × (8 + 12) × h = 120
½ × 20 × h = 120
10h = 120 → h = 12 cm
```

**Example 20**: In an isosceles trapezium, parallel sides are 20 cm and 30 cm, non-parallel sides are 13 cm each. Find area.
```
Difference in parallel sides = 30 - 20 = 10 cm
Half of difference = 5 cm (this forms base of right triangle with height)
Height = √(13² - 5²) = √(169 - 25) = √144 = 12 cm
Area = ½ × (20 + 30) × 12 = ½ × 50 × 12 = 300 cm²
```

## 3.6 General Quadrilateral

### Formula:
**Area** = ½ × diagonal × (sum of perpendiculars from opposite vertices to that diagonal)

### Solved Example:

**Example 21**: In a quadrilateral, diagonal = 12 cm, perpendiculars from other vertices are 5 cm and 7 cm. Find area.
```
Area = ½ × 12 × (5 + 7) = ½ × 12 × 12 = 72 cm²
```

## 3.7 MHT CET Shortcuts for Quadrilaterals

### 1. Rectangle Max/Min Problems
- For fixed perimeter, square has maximum area
- For fixed area, square has minimum perimeter

### 2. Rhombus vs Square
- Square is a special rhombus with all angles 90°
- Area of rhombus = a² sin θ (maximum when θ = 90°, i.e., square)

### 3. Trapezium Area Shortcut
If median (m) is given: Area = m × h

# SECTION 4: CIRCLES - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 4.1 Basic Circle Formulas

| Measure | Formula | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Circumference | 2πr | Also πd |
| Area | πr² | - |
| Diameter | 2r | d = 2r |
| Radius | d/2 | - |

### Important Values of π:
- π = 22/7 (for exact calculations)
- π ≈ 3.14 (for decimal answers)
- π ≈ 3.14159 (accurate)

### Solved Examples:

**Example 22**: Find circumference and area of circle with radius 7 cm.
```
Circumference = 2 × (22/7) × 7 = 44 cm
Area = (22/7) × 7² = (22/7) × 49 = 154 cm²
```

**Example 23**: Circumference of circle is 88 cm. Find its area.
```
2πr = 88
2 × (22/7) × r = 88
(44/7)r = 88
r = 88 × 7/44 = 14 cm
Area = πr² = (22/7) × 196 = 616 cm²
```

## 4.2 Circular Ring (Annulus)

### Formula:
**Area** = π(R² - r²) where R > r (outer radius > inner radius)

### Solved Example:

**Example 24**: Find area of circular ring with outer radius 10 cm, inner radius 8 cm.
```
Area = π(10² - 8²) = π(100 - 64) = 36π cm²
= 36 × 22/7 = 792/7 = 113.14 cm²
```

## 4.3 Sector of a Circle

### Complete Formula Sheet:

| Measure | Formula | When θ in degrees | When θ in radians |
|---------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Arc Length | (θ/360) × 2πr | θ in degrees | rθ (θ in radians) |
| Area of Sector | (θ/360) × πr² | θ in degrees | ½r²θ (θ in radians) |
| Perimeter of Sector | 2r + arc length | - | - |
| Chord Length | 2r sin(θ/2) | - | - |

### Important Points:
- θ is the central angle
- Sector is like a "pizza slice"
- Major sector has θ > 180°, minor sector has θ < 180°

### Solved Examples:

**Example 25**: Find area and arc length of sector with radius 14 cm and angle 60°.
```
Area = (60/360) × (22/7) × 14² 
     = (1/6) × (22/7) × 196 
     = (1/6) × 616 = 102.67 cm²

Arc length = (60/360) × 2 × (22/7) × 14 
           = (1/6) × 88 = 14.67 cm

Perimeter = 2r + arc length = 28 + 14.67 = 42.67 cm
```

**Example 26**: Arc length of sector is 22 cm, radius is 14 cm. Find angle.
```
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr
22 = (θ/360) × 2 × (22/7) × 14
22 = (θ/360) × 88
θ/360 = 22/88 = 1/4
θ = 90°
```

## 4.4 Segment of a Circle

### Formula:
**Area of segment** = Area of sector - Area of triangle

### For minor segment (θ < 180°):
Area = r²[ (πθ/360°) - (sin θ)/2 ]

### Solved Examples:

**Example 27**: For a circle of radius 10 cm with chord making 90° at center, find area of segment.
```
Area of sector = (90/360) × π × 100 = (1/4) × 100π = 25π cm²
Area of triangle = ½ × r² × sin θ = ½ × 100 × sin 90° = ½ × 100 × 1 = 50 cm²
Area of segment = 25π - 50 = 25(3.14) - 50 = 78.5 - 50 = 28.5 cm²
```

**Example 28**: Find area of segment when chord subtends 120° in circle of radius 7 cm.
```
Area of sector = (120/360) × (22/7) × 49 = (1/3) × 154 = 51.33 cm²
Area of triangle = ½ × 49 × sin 120° = 24.5 × (√3/2) = 24.5 × 0.866 = 21.22 cm²
Area of segment = 51.33 - 21.22 = 30.11 cm²
```

## 4.5 Important Circle Properties for MHT CET

### 1. Cyclic Quadrilateral
- Opposite angles sum = 180°
- Area = √[(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)] where s = (a+b+c+d)/2

### 2. Tangent Properties
- Tangent perpendicular to radius at point of contact
- Lengths of tangents from external point are equal

### 3. Chord Properties
- Perpendicular from center bisects chord
- Equal chords are equidistant from center

### 4. MHT CET Shortcuts

**Shortcut 1**: If a circle is inscribed in a square of side a:
- Radius of circle = a/2
- Area of circle = πa²/4
- Area between square and circle = a²(1 - π/4)

**Shortcut 2**: If a square is inscribed in a circle of radius r:
- Side of square = r√2
- Area of square = 2r²
- Area between circle and square = πr² - 2r² = r²(π - 2)

**Shortcut 3**: For sectors, remember:
- 60° sector → 1/6 of circle
- 90° sector → 1/4 of circle
- 120° sector → 1/3 of circle
- 180° sector → 1/2 of circle

# SECTION 5: CUBE - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 5.1 Complete Formula Sheet

| Measure | Formula | Derivation/Mnemonic |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| Volume | a³ | Side × Side × Side |
| Lateral Surface Area (LSA) | 4a² | Area of 4 side faces |
| Total Surface Area (TSA) | 6a² | Area of all 6 faces |
| Face Diagonal | a√2 | Pythagoras on one face |
| Space Diagonal | a√3 | Pythagoras in 3D |
| Edge | a | Given any measure, can find a |
| Volume from diagonal | (diagonal/√3)³ | - |

### Important Relations:
- TSA = 6 × (face area)
- LSA = 4 × (face area)
- Space diagonal = √3 × edge
- Face diagonal = √2 × edge

## 5.2 Solved Examples

**Example 29**: Find volume, TSA, LSA, and diagonals of cube with edge 5 cm.
```
Volume = 5³ = 125 cm³
TSA = 6 × 5² = 6 × 25 = 150 cm²
LSA = 4 × 5² = 4 × 25 = 100 cm²
Face diagonal = 5√2 = 5 × 1.414 = 7.07 cm
Space diagonal = 5√3 = 5 × 1.732 = 8.66 cm
```

**Example 30**: If volume of cube is 216 cm³, find its edge, TSA, and diagonals.
```
a³ = 216 ⇒ a = 6 cm (since 6³ = 216)
TSA = 6 × 6² = 6 × 36 = 216 cm²
Face diagonal = 6√2 cm
Space diagonal = 6√3 cm
```

**Example 31**: Total surface area of cube is 150 cm². Find its volume.
```
6a² = 150
a² = 25 → a = 5 cm
Volume = 5³ = 125 cm³
```

**Example 32**: Space diagonal of cube is 12√3 cm. Find its volume.
```
Space diagonal = a√3 = 12√3
Therefore a = 12 cm
Volume = 12³ = 1728 cm³
```

## 5.3 MHT CET Shortcuts for Cube

### 1. Relationship Between Measures
If edge = a, then:
- Face diagonal = 1.414a
- Space diagonal = 1.732a
- TSA = 6a²
- Volume = a³

### 2. If edge is doubled:
- Volume becomes 8 times
- Surface area becomes 4 times
- Diagonal becomes 2 times

### 3. If volume is doubled:
- Edge becomes ∛2 times ≈ 1.26 times
- Surface area becomes (∛2)² ≈ 1.59 times

### 4. Quick Calculations
- 3³ = 27, 4³ = 64, 5³ = 125, 6³ = 216, 7³ = 343, 8³ = 512, 9³ = 729, 10³ = 1000
- Memorize cubes up to 20 for speed

# SECTION 6: CUBOID - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 6.1 Complete Formula Sheet

| Measure | Formula | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Volume | l × b × h | Length × Breadth × Height |
| Lateral Surface Area (LSA) | 2h(l + b) | Area of 4 side walls |
| Total Surface Area (TSA) | 2(lb + bh + hl) | All 6 faces |
| Diagonal | √(l² + b² + h²) | Space diagonal |
| Face diagonal (l-b face) | √(l² + b²) | On length-breadth face |
| Face diagonal (l-h face) | √(l² + h²) | On length-height face |
| Face diagonal (b-h face) | √(b² + h²) | On breadth-height face |

### Important Points:
- All faces are rectangles
- Opposite faces are equal
- LSA excludes top and bottom

## 6.2 Solved Examples

**Example 33**: Find volume, TSA, LSA, and diagonal of cuboid with l=8 cm, b=6 cm, h=5 cm.
```
Volume = 8 × 6 × 5 = 240 cm³
TSA = 2(8×6 + 6×5 + 5×8) = 2(48 + 30 + 40) = 2 × 118 = 236 cm²
LSA = 2 × 5(8 + 6) = 10 × 14 = 140 cm²
Diagonal = √(8² + 6² + 5²) = √(64 + 36 + 25) = √125 = 5√5 cm ≈ 11.18 cm
```

**Example 34**: Find the maximum length of rod that can be placed in a room of dimensions 12 m × 9 m × 8 m.
```
Maximum length = diagonal = √(12² + 9² + 8²) 
                = √(144 + 81 + 64) 
                = √289 = 17 m
```

**Example 35**: TSA of cuboid is 126 cm², LSA is 70 cm², and base area is 20 cm². Find dimensions.
```
Let dimensions be l, b, h
Given: TSA = 2(lb + bh + hl) = 126 ...(1)
LSA = 2h(l + b) = 70 ...(2)
Base area = lb = 20 ...(3)

From (1): lb + bh + hl = 63
20 + h(l + b) = 63
h(l + b) = 43 ...(4)

From (2): 2h(l + b) = 70
h(l + b) = 35 ...(5)

From (4) and (5): 43 = 35 (Contradiction!)
This means given data is inconsistent - typical MHT CET trap!
```

**Example 36**: Volume of cuboid is 840 cm³, base area is 84 cm², and LSA is 360 cm². Find dimensions.
```
Volume = lbh = 840
Base area = lb = 84
Therefore h = 840/84 = 10 cm

LSA = 2h(l + b) = 360
2 × 10 × (l + b) = 360
20(l + b) = 360
l + b = 18

Now we have lb = 84 and l + b = 18
Solving: l and b are roots of x² - 18x + 84 = 0
Discriminant = 324 - 336 = -12 (No real solution!)
Another trap - always check if dimensions are possible
```

## 6.3 Special Cases

### Cube as Special Cuboid
When l = b = h, cuboid becomes cube

### Square-based Cuboid
When l = b ≠ h, formulas simplify:
- Volume = l²h
- TSA = 2(l² + 2lh)
- LSA = 4lh
- Diagonal = √(2l² + h²)

## 6.4 MHT CET Shortcuts for Cuboid

### 1. If all dimensions are increased by k times:
- Volume becomes k³ times
- Surface area becomes k² times
- Diagonal becomes k times

### 2. For a room (4 walls + ceiling/floor):
- Area of 4 walls = 2h(l + b) = LSA
- Area of ceiling = l × b
- Area of floor = l × b
- Total painting area (walls + ceiling) = 2h(l + b) + lb

### 3. Quick Diagonal Recognition:
- 3-4-5 triple: √(3²+4²) = 5
- 5-12-13: √(5²+12²) = 13
- 8-15-17: √(8²+15²) = 17
- For 3D, look for numbers that sum squares to perfect squares

### 4. Common Pythagorean Triples in 3D:
- 3,4,5 → diagonal = √50 = 5√2 ≈ 7.07
- 6,8,10 → diagonal = √200 = 10√2 ≈ 14.14
- 5,12,13 → diagonal = √338 ≈ 18.38

# SECTION 7: RIGHT CIRCULAR CYLINDER - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 7.1 Complete Formula Sheet

| Measure | Formula | Explanation |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Volume | πr²h | Area of base × height |
| Curved Surface Area (CSA) | 2πrh | Lateral surface (like label on a can) |
| Total Surface Area (TSA) | 2πr(h + r) | CSA + 2 × base area |
| Base Area | πr² | Area of circular base |
| Height | h | - |
| Radius | r | - |

### Important Points:
- Bases are circles of radius r
- Axis is perpendicular to bases
- If cut vertically, get rectangle of dimensions h × 2πr

## 7.2 Solved Examples

**Example 37**: Find volume, CSA, and TSA of cylinder with radius 7 cm, height 10 cm.
```
Volume = (22/7) × 7² × 10 = (22/7) × 49 × 10 = 22 × 7 × 10 = 1540 cm³
CSA = 2 × (22/7) × 7 × 10 = 2 × 22 × 10 = 440 cm²
TSA = 2 × (22/7) × 7 × (10 + 7) = 2 × 22 × 17 = 748 cm²
```

**Example 38**: Find height of cylinder whose volume is 1232 cm³ and radius is 7 cm.
```
πr²h = 1232
(22/7) × 49 × h = 1232
22 × 7 × h = 1232
154h = 1232
h = 1232/154 = 8 cm
```

**Example 39**: Find radius of cylinder whose CSA is 880 cm² and height is 20 cm.
```
2πrh = 880
2 × (22/7) × r × 20 = 880
(880/7) × r = 880
r/7 = 1
r = 7 cm
```

**Example 40**: TSA of cylinder is 462 cm², CSA is 308 cm². Find radius and height.
```
TSA = CSA + 2πr²
462 = 308 + 2πr²
2πr² = 154
πr² = 77
(22/7)r² = 77
r² = 77 × 7/22 = 539/22 = 24.5
r = √24.5 = 4.95 cm ≈ 5 cm

CSA = 2πrh = 308
2 × (22/7) × 5 × h = 308
(220/7)h = 308
h = 308 × 7/220 = 2156/220 = 9.8 cm
```

## 7.3 Hollow Cylinder

| Measure | Formula |
|---------|---------|
| Volume of material | πh(R² - r²) |
| Curved Surface Area (outer) | 2πRh |
| Curved Surface Area (inner) | 2πrh |
| Total Surface Area | 2πh(R + r) + 2π(R² - r²) |
| Area of each annular end | π(R² - r²) |

**Example 41**: Find volume of material in hollow cylinder of height 10 cm, outer radius 8 cm, inner radius 6 cm.
```
Volume = π × 10 × (8² - 6²) = π × 10 × (64 - 36) = π × 10 × 28 = 280π cm³
= 280 × 3.14 = 879.2 cm³
= 280 × 22/7 = 880 cm³ (exact)
```

## 7.4 MHT CET Shortcuts for Cylinder

### 1. Effect of Changing Dimensions:
- If radius doubles: Volume becomes 4 times, CSA becomes 2 times
- If height doubles: Volume becomes 2 times, CSA becomes 2 times
- If both radius and height double: Volume becomes 8 times, CSA becomes 4 times

### 2. Cylinder with Maximum Volume for Given Surface Area:
- For fixed TSA, volume is maximum when h = 2r
- For fixed CSA, volume is maximum when h = r

### 3. Common Values to Remember:
- For r = 7, h = 10: Volume = 1540, CSA = 440, TSA = 748
- For r = 14, h = 20: Volume = 12320, CSA = 1760, TSA = 2992
- For r = 3.5, h = 10: Volume = 385, CSA = 220, TSA = 297

### 4. When π cancels:
In ratio problems, π often cancels out. Look for opportunities to simplify.

# SECTION 8: RIGHT CIRCULAR CONE - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 8.1 Complete Formula Sheet

| Measure | Formula | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Slant Height (l) | l = √(r² + h²) | Pythagoras in right triangle |
| Volume | (1/3)πr²h | One-third of cylinder volume |
| Curved Surface Area (CSA) | πrl | Lateral surface |
| Total Surface Area (TSA) | πr(l + r) | CSA + base area |
| Base Area | πr² | - |
| Height | h | - |
| Radius | r | - |

### Important Relationships:
- l² = r² + h²
- Volume = (1/3) × (base area) × height
- CSA = πr√(r² + h²)

## 8.2 Solved Examples

**Example 42**: Find slant height, volume, CSA, and TSA of cone with radius 6 cm, height 8 cm.
```
Slant height l = √(6² + 8²) = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10 cm
Volume = (1/3) × (22/7) × 6² × 8 = (1/3) × (22/7) × 36 × 8
       = (1/3) × (22/7) × 288 = (22 × 288)/(21) = 6336/21 = 301.71 cm³
CSA = (22/7) × 6 × 10 = (22 × 60)/7 = 1320/7 = 188.57 cm²
TSA = (22/7) × 6 × (10 + 6) = (22/7) × 6 × 16 = (22 × 96)/7 = 2112/7 = 301.71 cm²
```

**Example 43**: Find height of cone whose volume is 1232 cm³ and radius is 7 cm.
```
(1/3)πr²h = 1232
(1/3) × (22/7) × 49 × h = 1232
(1/3) × 22 × 7 × h = 1232
(154/3)h = 1232
h = 1232 × 3/154 = 3696/154 = 24 cm
```

**Example 44**: Slant height of cone is 25 cm, radius is 7 cm. Find height and volume.
```
l² = r² + h²
25² = 7² + h²
625 = 49 + h²
h² = 576 → h = 24 cm
Volume = (1/3) × (22/7) × 49 × 24 = (1/3) × 22 × 7 × 24 = (1/3) × 3696 = 1232 cm³
```

**Example 45**: CSA of cone is 550 cm², radius is 7 cm. Find slant height and height.
```
πrl = 550
(22/7) × 7 × l = 550
22 × l = 550
l = 550/22 = 25 cm
h = √(l² - r²) = √(625 - 49) = √576 = 24 cm
```

## 8.3 Frustum of a Cone

When a cone is cut parallel to base, we get a frustum (smaller cone removed from top).

### Complete Formula Sheet for Frustum:

| Measure | Formula |
|---------|---------|
| Slant Height (l) | l = √[h² + (R - r)²] |
| Volume | (πh/3)(R² + Rr + r²) |
| Curved Surface Area | πl(R + r) |
| Total Surface Area | πl(R + r) + πR² + πr² |
| Height of original cone | H = hR/(R - r) |
| Height of removed cone | h' = hr/(R - r) |

### Solved Examples:

**Example 46**: Find volume of frustum of cone with height 12 cm, top radius 4 cm, bottom radius 10 cm.
```
Volume = (π × 12/3)(10² + 10×4 + 4²) = 4π(100 + 40 + 16) = 4π × 156 = 624π cm³
= 624 × 22/7 = 1961.14 cm³
```

**Example 47**: Find CSA of frustum with slant height 13 cm, top radius 4 cm, bottom radius 10 cm.
```
CSA = π × 13 × (10 + 4) = π × 13 × 14 = 182π cm²
= 182 × 22/7 = 572 cm²
```

## 8.4 MHT CET Shortcuts for Cone

### 1. Relationship Between r, h, l:
- 3-4-5 triangle: r=3, h=4, l=5
- 5-12-13 triangle: r=5, h=12, l=13
- 7-24-25 triangle: r=7, h=24, l=25
- 8-15-17 triangle: r=8, h=15, l=17

### 2. Volume Ratios:
- Volume of cone = 1/3 × volume of cylinder with same base and height
- If a cone and cylinder have same radius and height, volume ratio = 1:3

### 3. Effect of Changing Dimensions:
- If radius doubles (height constant): Volume becomes 4 times
- If height doubles (radius constant): Volume becomes 2 times
- If both double: Volume becomes 8 times

### 4. Maximum Volume Cone from a Cylinder:
Maximum cone volume from a cylinder = 1/3 × cylinder volume

### 5. Frustum Shortcut:
If a cone is cut at mid-height, the frustum volume = 7/8 of original cone volume

# SECTION 9: SPHERE - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 9.1 Complete Formula Sheet

| Measure | Formula | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Volume | (4/3)πr³ | Four-thirds pi r cubed |
| Surface Area | 4πr² | Four pi r squared |
| Diameter | 2r | - |
| Radius | r | - |

### Important Points:
- All points on surface equidistant from center
- Perfectly symmetrical
- Largest volume for given surface area

## 9.2 Solved Examples

**Example 48**: Find volume and surface area of sphere with radius 7 cm.
```
Volume = (4/3) × (22/7) × 7³ = (4/3) × (22/7) × 343
       = (4/3) × 22 × 49 = (4 × 22 × 49)/3 = 4312/3 = 1437.33 cm³
Surface Area = 4 × (22/7) × 7² = 4 × (22/7) × 49 = 4 × 22 × 7 = 616 cm²
```

**Example 49**: Find radius of sphere whose surface area is 616 cm².
```
4πr² = 616
4 × (22/7) × r² = 616
(88/7)r² = 616
r² = 616 × 7/88 = 4312/88 = 49
r = 7 cm
```

**Example 50**: Volume of sphere is (38808) cm³. Find its radius. (π = 22/7)
```
(4/3) × (22/7) × r³ = 38808
(88/21)r³ = 38808
r³ = 38808 × 21/88 = 38808 × 21 ÷ 88
38808 ÷ 88 = 441 (since 88×441 = 38808)
r³ = 441 × 21 = 9261
r = ∛9261 = 21 cm (since 21³ = 9261)
```

**Example 51**: If radius of sphere is doubled, find ratio of new volume to original.
```
Original volume = (4/3)πr³
New volume = (4/3)π(2r)³ = (4/3)π × 8r³ = 8 × original volume
Ratio = 8 : 1
```

## 9.3 Hemisphere

Half of a sphere with a circular base.

### Complete Formula Sheet:

| Measure | Formula | Explanation |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Volume | (2/3)πr³ | Half of sphere volume |
| Curved Surface Area (CSA) | 2πr² | Half of sphere surface area |
| Total Surface Area (TSA) | 3πr² | CSA + base area (πr²) |
| Base Area | πr² | Circular face |

### Solved Examples:

**Example 52**: Find volume, CSA, and TSA of hemisphere with radius 7 cm.
```
Volume = (2/3) × (22/7) × 7³ = (2/3) × (22/7) × 343
       = (2/3) × 22 × 49 = (2 × 22 × 49)/3 = 2156/3 = 718.67 cm³
CSA = 2 × (22/7) × 7² = 2 × (22/7) × 49 = 2 × 22 × 7 = 308 cm²
TSA = 3 × (22/7) × 7² = 3 × 22 × 7 = 462 cm²
```

**Example 53**: TSA of hemisphere is 462 cm². Find its radius.
```
3πr² = 462
3 × (22/7) × r² = 462
(66/7)r² = 462
r² = 462 × 7/66 = 3234/66 = 49
r = 7 cm
```

## 9.4 Spherical Shell

### Formula:
**Volume of material** = (4/3)π(R³ - r³)

**Example 54**: Find volume of material in spherical shell with outer radius 10 cm, inner radius 8 cm.
```
Volume = (4/3)π(10³ - 8³) = (4/3)π(1000 - 512) = (4/3)π × 488 = (1952/3)π cm³
= 1952 × 22/(3×7) = 1952 × 22/21 = 1952 × 1.0476 = 2044.9 cm³
```

## 9.5 MHT CET Shortcuts for Sphere

### 1. Relationship Between Measures:
- Surface area ∝ r²
- Volume ∝ r³
- If radius doubles: SA becomes 4×, Volume becomes 8×

### 2. Sphere vs Cube:
- Largest sphere in cube of side a: radius = a/2
- Largest cube in sphere of radius r: side = 2r/√3

### 3. Common Values to Remember:
| r | Surface Area (π=22/7) | Volume (π=22/7) |
|---|------------------------|------------------|
| 3.5 cm | 154 cm² | 179.67 cm³ |
| 7 cm | 616 cm² | 1437.33 cm³ |
| 10.5 cm | 1386 cm² | 4851 cm³ |
| 14 cm | 2464 cm² | 11498.67 cm³ |

### 4. Hemisphere TSA Shortcut:
TSA of hemisphere = 3πr² = CSA + Base area
If TSA given, divide by 3π to get r²

# SECTION 10: COMBINED SOLIDS - DETAILED ANALYSIS

## 10.1 Basic Principles

### Volume:
**Total volume** = Sum of volumes of individual solids

### Surface Area:
**Total surface area** = Sum of areas of all **exposed** surfaces
- Internal contact surfaces are NOT included
- Only surfaces visible from outside count

## 10.2 Common Combinations

### Type 1: Cylinder with Hemisphere on Top (Capsule shape)

**Example 55**: A solid is formed by a cylinder of height 10 cm and radius 7 cm with a hemisphere of same radius on top. Find total volume and surface area.

```
Volume:
Volume of cylinder = π × 7² × 10 = 490π cm³
Volume of hemisphere = (2/3)π × 7³ = (2/3)π × 343 = (686/3)π cm³
Total volume = 490π + (686/3)π = (1470π + 686π)/3 = (2156π)/3 cm³
= 2156 × 22/(3×7) = 2156 × 22/21 = 2156 × 1.0476 = 2258.67 cm³

Surface Area:
Cylinder CSA = 2πrh = 2π × 7 × 10 = 140π cm²
Hemisphere CSA = 2πr² = 2π × 49 = 98π cm²
Base of cylinder (if on ground, not included) - assume open at bottom
Total exposed area = 140π + 98π = 238π cm² = 238 × 22/7 = 748 cm²
```

### Type 2: Cylinder with Two Hemispheres (Football shape)

**Example 56**: A solid consists of a cylinder of height 14 cm, radius 7 cm with hemispheres of same radius on both ends. Find total volume.

```
Volume of cylinder = π × 49 × 14 = 686π cm³
Volume of two hemispheres = 2 × (2/3)π × 343 = (4/3)π × 343 = (1372/3)π cm³
Total volume = 686π + (1372/3)π = (2058π + 1372π)/3 = (3430π)/3 cm³
= 3430 × 22/(3×7) = 3430 × 22/21 = 3593.33 cm³
```

### Type 3: Cone on Cylinder

**Example 57**: A tent is in form of cylinder of diameter 14 m and height 5 m, surmounted by a cone of same base radius and height 3 m. Find canvas required.

```
Radius = 7 m
Cylinder CSA = 2πrh = 2π × 7 × 5 = 70π m²
Slant height of cone l = √(7² + 3²) = √(49 + 9) = √58 ≈ 7.62 m
Cone CSA = πrl = π × 7 × 7.62 = 53.34π m²
Total canvas = 70π + 53.34π = 123.34π m² = 123.34 × 3.14 = 387.3 m²
```

### Type 4: Cube with Hemisphere on Top

**Example 58**: A cube of edge 14 cm has a hemisphere of diameter 14 cm carved on top. Find total surface area.

```
Cube TSA (without top) = 5a² = 5 × 196 = 980 cm² (since top is removed)
Hemisphere CSA = 2πr² = 2π × 49 = 98π cm²
Base of hemisphere (circle of radius 7 cm) = πr² = 49π cm²
But this circular area is NOT added as it's where hemisphere meets cube
Total exposed area = 980 + 98π = 980 + 308 = 1288 cm²
```

## 10.3 MHT CET Shortcuts for Combined Solids

### 1. Identify Contact Surfaces:
Always identify which surfaces are in contact - they are not included in TSA

### 2. Common Contact Scenarios:
- Hemisphere on cylinder: Circular face of hemisphere and top of cylinder are in contact
- Cone on cylinder: Circular base of cone and top of cylinder are in contact
- Two cubes joined: One face of each cube is in contact

### 3. Formula for Common Combinations:
- **Cylinder + Hemisphere** (open at bottom): TSA = 2πrh + 2πr²
- **Cylinder + Hemisphere** (closed bottom): TSA = 2πrh + 2πr² + πr² = 2πrh + 3πr²
- **Cylinder + Cone**: TSA = 2πrh + πrl + πr² (if bottom included)

# SECTION 11: MELTING AND RECASTING PROBLEMS

## 11.1 Basic Principle

**Golden Rule**: When one solid is melted and recast into another shape, **volume remains constant**.

Volume of original solid = Volume of new solid(s)

## 11.2 Types of Problems

### Type 1: One solid to another single solid

**Example 59**: A metallic sphere of radius 6 cm is melted and recast into a cylinder of height 8 cm. Find radius of cylinder.

```
Volume of sphere = (4/3)π × 6³ = (4/3)π × 216 = 288π cm³
Volume of cylinder = πr² × 8 = 8πr²
Equating: 8πr² = 288π
8r² = 288
r² = 36
r = 6 cm
```

**Example 60**: A cube of edge 11 cm is melted to form a cylinder of height 10 cm. Find radius of cylinder.

```
Volume of cube = 11³ = 1331 cm³
Volume of cylinder = πr² × 10 = 10πr²
10πr² = 1331
r² = 1331/(10π) = 1331/(10 × 3.14) = 1331/31.4 = 42.39
r = √42.39 = 6.51 cm
```

### Type 2: One solid to multiple small solids

**Example 61**: How many spherical bullets of radius 2 cm can be made from a solid cube of edge 11 cm?

```
Volume of cube = 11³ = 1331 cm³
Volume of one bullet = (4/3)π × 2³ = (4/3)π × 8 = (32π)/3 cm³
Number of bullets = Volume of cube / Volume of one bullet
= 1331 / [(32π)/3] = 1331 × 3 / (32π)
= 3993 / (32 × 22/7) = 3993 × 7 / (32 × 22)
= 27951 / 704 = 39.7 ≈ 39 bullets (whole number)
```

**Example 62**: A cone of height 24 cm and radius 6 cm is melted and recast into spheres of radius 1.5 cm. Find number of spheres.

```
Volume of cone = (1/3)π × 6² × 24 = (1/3)π × 36 × 24 = 288π cm³
Volume of one sphere = (4/3)π × (1.5)³ = (4/3)π × 3.375 = 4.5π cm³
Number of spheres = 288π / 4.5π = 288/4.5 = 64
```

### Type 3: Wire drawing problems

**Example 63**: A copper sphere of radius 3 cm is drawn into a wire of diameter 0.4 cm. Find length of wire.

```
Volume of sphere = (4/3)π × 27 = 36π cm³
Wire is a cylinder of radius 0.2 cm
Volume of wire = π × (0.2)² × l = 0.04πl
Equating: 0.04πl = 36π
0.04l = 36
l = 36/0.04 = 900 cm = 9 m
```

### Type 4: Mixed metals/Alloys

**Example 64**: Two spheres of radii 3 cm and 4 cm are melted to form a single sphere. Find radius of new sphere.

```
Volume of first sphere = (4/3)π × 27 = 36π cm³
Volume of second sphere = (4/3)π × 64 = (256/3)π cm³
Total volume = 36π + (256/3)π = (108π + 256π)/3 = (364π)/3 cm³

Volume of new sphere = (4/3)πR³
(4/3)πR³ = (364π)/3
4R³/3 = 364/3
4R³ = 364
R³ = 91
R = ∛91 ≈ 4.5 cm
```

## 11.3 MHT CET Shortcuts for Melting Problems

### 1. Cancel Common Factors:
Always cancel π, 4/3, 1/3 etc. before solving

### 2. For Multiple Small Solids:
Number = (Volume of big) / (Volume of small)
Always check if answer needs to be integer (whole number)

### 3. Common Cube Roots to Memorize:
- ∛8 = 2
- ∛27 = 3
- ∛64 = 4
- ∛125 = 5
- ∛216 = 6
- ∛343 = 7
- ∛512 = 8
- ∛729 = 9
- ∛1000 = 10
- ∛1331 = 11
- ∛1728 = 12

### 4. Common Sphere Volume Ratios:
- For radii in ratio a:b, volumes in ratio a³:b³
- If radius doubles, volume becomes 8 times

# SECTION 12: SURFACE AREA AND VOLUME RATIOS

## 12.1 Effect of Changing Dimensions

### For Similar Figures:
If dimensions are scaled by factor k:
- **Area** scales by factor k²
- **Volume** scales by factor k³

### Solved Examples:

**Example 65**: If the radius of a sphere is doubled, how does its volume change?
```
Original volume = (4/3)πr³
New volume = (4/3)π(2r)³ = (4/3)π × 8r³ = 8 × original volume
Volume becomes 8 times
```

**Example 66**: If each edge of a cube is doubled, find ratio of new surface area to original.
```
Original TSA = 6a²
New TSA = 6(2a)² = 6 × 4a² = 24a²
Ratio = 24a² : 6a² = 4 : 1
```

**Example 67**: If radius of cylinder is halved and height is doubled, find change in volume.
```
Original volume = πr²h
New volume = π(r/2)² × (2h) = π × (r²/4) × 2h = πr²h/2 = half of original
Volume becomes 50% of original
```

**Example 68**: If dimensions of cuboid are increased by 10% each, find percentage increase in volume.
```
Original volume = l × b × h
New dimensions = 1.1l, 1.1b, 1.1h
New volume = 1.1l × 1.1b × 1.1h = 1.331 × (l × b × h)
Increase = 0.331 × 100 = 33.1%
```

## 12.2 Maximum/Minimum Problems

### Type 1: Largest solid that can be carved from another

**Example 69**: Find maximum volume of cone that can be carved out of a cube of edge 12 cm.

```
For maximum cone, base radius = side/2 = 6 cm, height = side = 12 cm
Volume = (1/3)π × 6² × 12 = (1/3)π × 36 × 12 = 144π cm³
= 144 × 22/7 = 452.57 cm³
```

**Example 70**: Find volume of largest sphere that can be cut from a cube of edge 10 cm.

```
Sphere diameter = edge of cube = 10 cm, so radius = 5 cm
Volume = (4/3)π × 5³ = (4/3)π × 125 = (500π)/3 cm³
= 500 × 22/(3×7) = 11000/21 = 523.8 cm³
```

**Example 71**: Find volume of largest cylinder that can be cut from a cube of edge 14 cm.

```
For maximum cylinder, radius = side/2 = 7 cm, height = side = 14 cm
Volume = π × 7² × 14 = π × 49 × 14 = 686π cm³
= 686 × 22/7 = 2156 cm³
```

### Type 2: Maximum volume for given surface area

**Example 72**: For a cylinder with fixed surface area, when is volume maximum?

```
For fixed TSA = 2πr² + 2πrh = constant
Volume is maximum when h = 2r
Proof involves differentiation (not in syllabus but result useful)
```

## 12.3 MHT CET Shortcuts for Ratios

### 1. Percentage Change Shortcut:
If dimension increases by x%, new dimension = (100 + x)/100 times
New volume = [(100 + x)/100]³ times
Percentage increase = [(100 + x)³/100² - 1] × 100

### 2. Common Percentage Changes:
| % Increase in side | % Increase in area | % Increase in volume |
|--------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 10% | 21% | 33.1% |
| 20% | 44% | 72.8% |
| 25% | 56.25% | 95.3% |
| 50% | 125% | 237.5% |

### 3. If dimension is halved:
Area becomes 1/4, Volume becomes 1/8

### 4. For similar solids:
Ratio of volumes = (ratio of corresponding sides)³
Ratio of surface areas = (ratio of corresponding sides)²

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Unit Confusion**: Always convert to same unit before calculation
2. **Radius vs Diameter**: Check whether given is radius or diameter
3. **CSA vs TSA**: Read carefully what is asked
4. **π Value**: Use 22/7 unless decimals required
5. **Including Contact Surfaces**: In combined solids, don't add touching surfaces
6. **Heron's Formula**: Remember to take square root at the end
7. **Cone Volume**: Don't forget the 1/3 factor
8. **Hemisphere TSA**: Remember to add base area (πr²) to CSA
